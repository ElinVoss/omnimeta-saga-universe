- **Supply baseline**: 60% of minimum civilian requirement (post-siege scarcity)
- **Demand categories**: Medical triage (siege wounded), structural repairs, population support, military readiness
- **Decision mechanism**: Ration board votes on allocation percentages
- **Physical tracking**: Ration seals stamp each distribution unit; broken seals indicate fraud/theft
- **Stamper function**: Official allocation authority; broken stampers force revision of ledgers
- **Ledger documentation**: Every allocation decision recorded with date, recipient, quantity, authorizer signature

- **Scenario 1**: Vote to allocate more resources to military (corridor defense) → civilian rations reduced → malnutrition casualties increase

- **Scenario 3**: Vote to maintain equity → no faction prioritized → all weakened → vulnerability increases
- **Key insight**: No allocation choice prevents harm; all create casualties through different mechanisms
- **Jhace's burden**: Bears responsibility for allocation choices despite having no good options

- **Mobel decision**: Remove civic obstruction; assassination achieves tactical goal
- **Method**: During patrol between resistance and Mobel zones; made to appear like corridor war casualty
- **Plausible deniability**: Multiple factions could be blamed; investigation leads nowhere
- **Consequence**: Civic authority collapses further; god-vessels fill governance vacuum

### Corridor Wars (Attrition Mechanics)

- **Scale**: Multiple smaller battles instead of coordinated siege
- **Intensity**: Lower than siege but constant; no recovery periods between engagements
- **Duration**: Hours to days of position-holding before tactical repositioning
- **Casualties**: Slow accumulation instead of catastrophic events
- **Exhaustion**: All sides operating at minimum capacity; no reserves available
- **Winner**: None; stalemate emerging; mutual recognition of mutual inability to advance
